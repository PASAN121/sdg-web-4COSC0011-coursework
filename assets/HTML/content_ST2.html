Introduction to Deforestation

Forests cover approximately 31% of the Earth's total land area, yet every year an estimated 10 million hectares of forest are lost. Deforestation — the large-scale removal or clearing of forest land — is one of the most urgent environmental crises facing the planet today. It sits at the very centre of SDG 15, which calls for the protection, restoration, and sustainable use of terrestrial ecosystems. Unlike natural disturbances such as wildfires, deforestation is almost entirely driven by human activity. When trees are felled, the consequences ripple through the entire ecological system: soil erodes, water cycles are disrupted, biodiversity collapses, and enormous quantities of carbon stored in the trees are released into the atmosphere. Understanding deforestation is not just an academic exercise — it is a call to action. By recognising the causes and consequences, we can make smarter individual decisions and support policies that protect forests for future generations.

Causes of Deforestation

The single largest driver of deforestation globally is agriculture. As the world's population grows and demand for food increases, forests are cleared to create farmland — particularly for cattle ranching, soy farming, and palm oil plantations. In tropical regions like the Amazon and Southeast Asia, this has resulted in the destruction of some of the most biodiverse ecosystems on Earth.

Both legal and illegal logging operations remove vast areas of forest for timber, paper, and wood products. Illegal logging is particularly damaging because it often occurs in protected areas and is difficult to regulate. The construction of roads, dams, and urban settlements fragments and destroys forest habitats. A new road through a forest does not just remove the trees it cuts through — it opens the surrounding area to further human activity, accelerating deforestation in a ripple effect. Chango-Cañaveral et al. (2025) highlight how poorly planned infrastructure development in ecologically sensitive regions can permanently damage biodiversity and undermine SDG 15 targets.

Consequences of Deforestation

Loss of Biodiversity

Forests are home to more than 80% of terrestrial species. When a forest is cleared, species lose their habitat. Many cannot survive in fragmented or degraded landscapes and face local or global extinction. According to Guo (2025), biodiversity loss linked to habitat destruction is one of the key indicators tracked under SDG 15 progress reporting.

Climate Change

Forests act as carbon sinks, absorbing CO2 from the atmosphere. Deforestation not only removes this absorption capacity but also releases the stored carbon. It is estimated that deforestation and land-use change account for around 10–12% of global greenhouse gas emissions.

Disruption of Water Cycles

Trees play a critical role in regulating rainfall and preventing soil erosion. When forests are removed, rain runs off quickly rather than being absorbed, leading to floods in the wet season and droughts in the dry season. Communities that depend on forest-fed rivers for drinking water and irrigation are among the most severely affected.

What Is Being Done? Conservation Responses

International Agreements

The Paris Agreement, the Convention on Biological Diversity, and the UN Decade on Ecosystem Restoration (2021–2030) all include commitments to reduce deforestation and restore degraded forests. Under SDG 15, Target 15.2 specifically calls for halting deforestation and significantly increasing forest restoration by 2020, with further targets extending to 2030.

National Forest Protection Laws

Many countries have enacted laws to protect forests, establish protected areas, and regulate logging. However, enforcement is often weak, especially in regions with high corruption or limited resources. Strengthening governance and supporting local communities in forest stewardship are critical for the success of these laws.

Reforestation and Ecological Restoration

Planting trees and restoring degraded landscapes is central to reversing deforestation. Reeya et al. (2024) outline ecological restoration frameworks specifically designed to address the kinds of landscape degradation caused by deforestation, emphasising the need for locally adapted, community-supported approaches.

What You Can Do as a Student

Every person plays a role in the demand that drives deforestation. Here are five realistic actions students can take: Choose recycled paper products: notebooks, textbooks, and packaging all have sustainable alternatives. Reduce consumption of products linked to deforestation: palm oil is found in many processed foods and cosmetics; check labels and opt for certified sustainable versions.Support reforestation initiatives: many organisations allow online donations to plant trees; some, like Trees for the Future, offer student volunteer programmes. Spread informed awareness: share accurate facts about deforestation on social media and challenge misinformation when you see it. Advocate on campus: push for your university or school to adopt sustainable paper and procurement policies

'My actions are too small to matter'

Individual actions matter both directly and indirectly. Directly, they reduce demand. Indirectly, they signal to businesses and policymakers that consumers care about sustainability. Market and policy changes have consistently followed shifts in consumer behaviour

'It is too expensive to be sustainable'

Many sustainable choices are free or cheaper — eating less meat, buying second-hand, using the library instead of buying new books, cycling instead of driving. Start with the free or low-cost changes and build from there.

'I do not know enough to act'

You do not need to be an expert to take action. Reeya et al. (2024) emphasise that community-based restoration programmes are specifically designed to involve participants with no prior environmental knowledge — the skills are taught, and the act of participation is itself educational.

Conclusion

Deforestation is not an inevitable consequence of development it is a choice, and it can be reversed. The tools, knowledge, and international frameworks to protect and restore forests already exist. What is needed now is the collective will to use them. As students, each of us can contribute to that shift through our daily choices, our voices, and the careers we choose to build.
