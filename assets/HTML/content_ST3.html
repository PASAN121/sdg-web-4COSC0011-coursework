What Is Land Degradation?

Land degradation refers to the deterioration in the quality of land, including its soil, vegetation, and the water systems that depend on it, to a point where the land can no longer perform its ecological or productive functions effectively. It is one of the most widespread environmental challenges of our time, affecting an estimated 3.2 billion people globally.

SDG 15 specifically targets land degradation under Goal 15.3, which commits to achieving land degradation neutrality meaning that any new land degradation must be balanced or outweighed by land restoration elsewhere. Despite this commitment, progress has been slow and degradation continues at an alarming rate.

Land degradation is often described as a 'slow disaster' it lacks the dramatic visibility of a flood or wildfire, but its cumulative effects are equally devastating to food security, biodiversity, and human wellbeing.

Causes of Land Degradation

Unsustainable Agricultural Practices

Intensive farming that relies on heavy machinery, chemical fertilisers, and monoculture cropping strips nutrients from the soil, compacts the land, and destroys the microorganism communities that keep soil healthy. Over time, soil loses its structure and fertility, reducing crop yields and increasing the need for even more chemical inputs — a vicious cycle.

Deforestation

Trees and their root systems hold soil in place and regulate its moisture content. When forests are removed, the exposed soil is vulnerable to both wind and water erosion. Heavy rainfall on bare hillsides carries topsoil away in rivers, leaving behind infertile, compacted land that is difficult to farm or restore.

Urbanisation and Infrastructures

Sealing land under concrete and asphalt for roads, buildings, and car parks permanently removes it from the natural ecosystem. Poorly planned development without environmental impact assessment can accelerate erosion and pollution of surrounding land (Chango-Cañaveral et al., 2025).

Consequences of Land Degradation

Biodiversity Loss

Healthy soil is itself an ecosystem, home to bacteria, fungi, worms, insects, and countless other organisms. Land degradation destroys these communities, reducing the soil's ability to support plant life and, in turn, the animals that depend on those plants. Guo (2025) notes that soil biodiversity is a critical but often overlooked indicator within SDG 15 monitoring frameworks.

Desertification

In dry regions, land degradation can tip into desertification — the process by which fertile land becomes desert. This is particularly acute in sub-Saharan Africa and parts of Central Asia, where millions of people are already displaced or at risk due to advancing desert conditions.

Loss of Food Security & Water Scarcity

Approximately 52% of the land used for agriculture worldwide is moderately or severely degraded. As soil fertility declines, crop yields fall, making food less available and more expensive affecting the poorest communities most severely. Degraded soils cannot hold water effectively, leading to increased runoff, reduced groundwater recharge, and more frequent water shortages in surrounding areas. This creates a self-reinforcing cycle of degradation.

Restoration Approaches

Restoring degraded land is challenging but achievable, and several proven approaches are being scaled up globally.

Sustainable Land Management

SLM practices include crop rotation, cover cropping, reduced tillage, and the integration of trees and shrubs into farmland (known as agroforestry). These approaches improve soil health, increase water retention, and enhance biodiversity while maintaining agricultural productivity.

Revegetation and Reforestation

Planting native vegetation on degraded land protects the soil from erosion, returns nutrients to the ecosystem, and creates habitat for wildlife. Reeya et al. (2024) present a detailed framework for ecological restoration in degraded Mediterranean landscapes that is directly applicable to SDG 15 land neutrality targets — demonstrating how restoration must be scientifically designed to match local conditions

Legal Frameworks and Land Rights

Clear, secure land tenure gives farmers and communities an incentive to invest in long-term land health. In many countries, insecure land rights lead to short-term exploitation rather than sustainable management.

What You Can Do as a Student

Land degradation may seem distant from daily student life, but your choices as a consumer and citizen have a direct connection.

  • Support local and organic farmers: local food systems that use sustainable practices protect soils and reduce the need for chemical inputs.
  • Volunteer for soil and land restoration projects: many conservation organisations run weekend planting and restoration events that are open to students.
  • Learn about land rights : educate yourself about the relationship between land ownership, indigenous rights, and environmental protection.
  • Advocate for sustainable procurement on campus : push your university to source sustainably produced food and paper products.

Conclusion

Land is not a renewable resource on any human timescale once topsoil is lost to erosion or desertification, it can take centuries to rebuild naturally. Achieving SDG 15's land degradation neutrality target requires a combination of policy, science, and individual behaviour change. Students have a unique opportunity to advocate for, and model, the kind of sustainable relationship with land that future generations will depend on.
